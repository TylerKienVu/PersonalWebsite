User Research

 User Interviews
User interviews gave me a good place to start in terms of figuring out what I should prioritize with my time in order to get the best user experience possible. I asked questions like "What makes a website 'good' to you?" and "What would you look for on a personal website as a hiring manager?". What I concluded from the responses was that people wanted simple, intuitive, and cohesive design with strong influential content.
 Competitor Analysis
A Competitor Analysis is the the process of researching similar products in order to know what features are a must have and what features could be added to make your product unique. I made sure to analyze front end web developer portfolios to get a good idea on the standard I would need to reach in order to be at the level I want. I took note of design decisions that grabbed my attention and tried to apply it to my own portfolio.
 Feature Value Matrix
Creating a Feature Value Matrix is the practice of listing all of the design decisions you want to implement and weighing them against each other. The reasoning behind the features I chose goes back to the goal of having a simple, intuitive, and cohesive design. I ended up having scope creep while creating the portfolio but I am happy with the finished product.
 Feature Prioritization
I put heavy prioritization on the essential features of the portfolio like the "About" and "Experience" sections. Once I had the main content of the portfolio, I then could add more design in order to make the portfolio further stand out.
 

User Testing

 User Stories
User stories were more important than I realized after I created them. I put myself in the shoes of a hiring manager and walked through the top 10 things I would want to see. It made me realize that my portfolio was missing some essential components which could be the difference between a recruiter giving me a chance or not.
 Cognitive Walkthrough
A Cognitive Walkthrough is an in person examination of a user's thought process when going through an interface. This process is essential for the success of a design because it reveals many holes that you did not see when originally implementing it. My Cognitive Walkthroughs taught me that people have different mindsets when going through a site in terms of what they look for and how they look for it.
 User Test
For my user testing I decided to do a click test and an impression test. My click test task was to click a link on the navigation bar that you believed would take you to my resume. The results surprised me because all the users picked different areas. My second test was a landing page impression test. People seemed to like my old landing page but there was still room for improvement such as including my name.
 

Conclusion

 Summary of Findings
 Conducting User Research was a great learning experience because it allowed me to gain insight on what other's wanted from a personal portfolio. I ended up getting alot more information from the interviews than I was expecting. The top 3 things that the users I interviewed wanted from a personal portfolio is simple design, intuitive design, and strong content. The user's desires ended up correlating with my original intentions on what I wanted for my portfolio so I had no issues there. My biggest concern was that I would not design correctly to fit my intentions. I knew that I would probably have to go through many trials and errors to figure out what I feel works the best.

 The User Testing portion of the project made me rethink my whole design. The User Tests showed that my "First draft" of my portfolio was not as intuitive as I originally thought and that I was missing some extremely basic and essential elements like my name. I got so caught up in the design aspect of the portfolio that I forgot to include basic information. Simply put, my portfolio needed alot more work in every area. The content and design were severely lacking.
 Incorporation of Findings
 When I started creating my "First draft", I tried to tailor the design to fit the information that I got from the User Interviews. I soon realized that with my limited knowledge of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, what the users wanted was not an easy goal to accomplish. I had no idea how to make what I was picturing in my head and ended up creating very lack-luster substitutes. I ran into numerous problems that I couldn't figure out for the longest time. But I eventually learned and now, hopefully, I won't make the same mistake twice. The "first draft" of my personal portfolio turned out alright in my opinion given the limited experience I had with front-end web development but it definitely was not enough.

 After gathering more data from User Testing, it confirmed my doubts that my "First draft" was not where it needed to be. I also had two underlying problems that I had to debate for a while whether I wanted to address. My first problem was that I originally designed my portfolio with Bootstrap 3 but later realized that Bootstrap 4 was much better for what I wanted to do. My second problem was that I designed my portfolio desktop-first which made the queries very clunky to work with. So all that said, I made a very painstaking decision to scratch my old portfolio and create a new one with the updated Bootstrap 4 framework and mobile-first design. The process of adopting the updated framework took me alot more time than I would like to admit but I definitely believe it was worth it. I wanted to make sure that my portfolio was up-to-date because I plan on using it in the future. With the addition of Bootstrap 4 and mobile-first design, things progressed alot more smoothly. I tackled my previous problems of not having strong content and intuitive design and I am happy with the results.
